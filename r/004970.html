Business

Silla Trading, founded in 1967 and listed on KOSPI in 1976, operates across five segments: deep-sea fisheries, seafood distribution, steel trading, agricultural brokerage, and QSR food service. The fisheries segment runs six purse-seine vessels targeting skipjack and yellowfin tuna for canning in the Central and Western Pacific, plus nine longliners supplying premium sashimi-grade tuna, with catches marketed through overseas joint ventures. The seafood distribution unit handles frozen fish including pollock, while the steel segment sources and resells products from POSCO, Hyundai Steel, China, Japan, and India. The agricultural brokerage segment operates at Garak Market in Seoul. For QSR, wholly owned subsidiary NLC holds the exclusive domestic Popeyes franchise, deploying self-ordering kiosks and Naver pre-order integration to improve store economics, with a stated long-term target of over 330 stores within a decade. Early-stage capital support from the parent company underpins NLC's expansion. The controlling shareholder is Silla Holdings with a 40.18% stake; including eleven related parties, the group controls 64.42% of the shares outstanding.

Earnings

Annual revenues grew steadily from KRW 432.6 bn in 2022 to KRW 496.1 bn in 2024, with operating margins holding in a tight 3.2–3.3% band over the same period. In 2024, operating profit reached KRW 16.4 bn (OPM 3.3%) and net profit attributable to owners surged to KRW 39.6 bn — more than double the KRW 17.2 bn in 2023 — backed by operating cash flow of KRW 66.8 bn. The year 2025 was a sharp reversal: revenue fell 8.3% YoY to KRW 454.7 bn, operating profit swung to a loss of KRW 1.57 bn, and attributable net income collapsed to KRW 4.64 bn. Quarterly analysis reveals that Q2 2025 bore the brunt — an operating loss of KRW 1.56 bn alongside an attributable net loss of KRW 11.56 bn — followed by a deeper Q3 2025 operating loss of KRW 3.16 bn, both driven by weak catches and falling tuna prices. Annual EPS declined to KRW 294 in 2025 from KRW 2,509 in 2024. In 2026 Q1, despite a revenue decline to KRW 85.9 bn from KRW 117.7 bn a year earlier, operating profit surged to KRW 4.47 bn (~5.2% OPM) and attributable net income reached KRW 11.60 bn, sending a clear signal of structural cost improvement.

Industry

The domestic deep-sea fisheries sector is an oligopoly dominated by a handful of large operators — Silla Trading, Sajo Industries, and Dongwon Industries — where high barriers to entry such as vessel fleets, fishing licenses, and operational networks protect incumbent market positions. The Western and Central Pacific Fisheries Commission (WCPFC) continues tightening catch quotas and operational day limits, placing a structural ceiling on production growth. Global skipjack tuna prices are highly volatile, driven by supply-demand dynamics in key importing countries including the U.S., Japan, and Thailand; U.S. reciprocal tariff measures introduced in 2025 exerted direct downward pressure on fish prices. The U.S. Marine Mammal Protection Act (effective January 2026) now requires exporters to hold admissibility certification for their fishing methods, adding a compliance layer that represents a new export risk. The steel distribution segment faces persistent margin compression from Chinese low-cost steel inflows, while the domestic QSR market is intensely competitive, requiring sustained investment and time for new brands to establish themselves. Ultimately, whether the core fisheries segment recovers to its historical role as the group's cash engine is the pivotal question for overall financial health.
