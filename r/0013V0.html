Business

Samjin Food is a fish cake (eomuk) specialist that traces its origins to a small workshop in Busan's Yeongdo district in 1953 and is now managed by its third-generation owner, Park Yong-jun; the company made its KOSDAQ debut on December 22, 2025. The product portfolio (2024 basis) spans soup/stew-use (37.6%), side-dish-use (21.7%), packaged snacks (20.7%), gift sets (14.8%), and ready-to-eat snacks (8.1%), covering a broad range of consumption occasions from everyday cooking to premium gifting. Sales channels are well diversified across retail (29.1%), B2B (27.0%), company-owned and franchise stores (21.4%), online (19.5%), and exports (2.8%), reducing single-channel concentration risk. The company's defining competitive advantage is full vertical integration—directly procuring raw surimi from overseas producers, conducting proprietary formulation R&D, and managing manufacturing, logistics, and branding entirely in-house—a rare structure in the Korean fish cake industry. This model sustains a roughly 2x price premium over competitors alongside revenue volumes approximately 5x larger, and has earned the #1 ranking in the K-BPI seafood processing category for five consecutive years. Production is anchored at two Busan facilities—the Jangrim Plant and the Gamcheon (Amazing Factory) Plant—with combined annual capacity of 11,104 tonnes. The company pioneered Korea's first fish cake bakery retail concept in 2013, opened its first overseas outlet in Singapore in 2017, and launched the 'Samjin Eomukdang' franchise model in 2021, repositioning fish cake from a commodity ingredient to a lifestyle brand experience. It currently operates fish cake bakery stores in Singapore, Indonesia, and Vietnam and exports to 13 countries, with IPO proceeds earmarked primarily for Jangrim Plant capacity expansion (targeting ~25% increase) and logistics system upgrades.

Outlook

Samjin is concurrently pursuing multiple international distribution channels: U.S. Costco and Walmart entry (building on existing H-Mart penetration), Costco supply and retail store openings in Taiwan and China in 2026, and Korean-community store channel expansion in Europe—all aligned with the 30%-overseas-revenue-in-five-years target. The macro environment is supportive: K-pop and K-content are elevating K-food awareness globally, and fish cake—a high-protein, low-fat seafood product—aligns with global food mega-trends including healthy pleasure eating and HMR adoption. Domestically, the company plans to raise the share of high-ASP premium gift sets (priced up to KRW 100,000) and expand its premium ready-to-eat lineup to drive further margin improvement. Completion of the Jangrim factory capacity expansion (+25%) using IPO proceeds should alleviate peak-season (Chuseok/winter) supply shortfalls and unlock incremental revenue leverage, supported by concurrent logistics system upgrades. Given the structural difficulty of rapidly scaling overseas revenues from ~2.8% toward 30%, the market reception of the shelf-stable product and the tangible progress on distribution partnerships in the U.S. and China will be the decisive variables shaping the 2026–2027 growth trajectory.

Bull case

K-Food Megatrend and Rising Global Demand for Seafood Protein

The global K-food wave driven by K-pop and K-drama is generating structural demand tailwinds, and fish cake—a high-protein, low-fat seafood product—fits squarely within global food mega-trends around healthy pleasure eating and HMR adoption. Korea's food export value grew from USD 6.06 billion (2016) to USD 10.66 billion (2024) per MAFRA data, and fish cake holds credible potential to become K-food's next breakout category following ramen and seaweed. Currently exporting to 13 countries, Samjin's planned shelf-stable fish cake launch will extend the addressable export market to regions lacking cold-chain infrastructure, representing a structural inflection point. Should K-street-food brand awareness accelerate globally, the upside to the medium-term overseas revenue trajectory could be meaningfully underpriced in the current stock.

Full Vertical Integration and a Durable Premium Brand Moat

Samjin's vertically integrated model—spanning raw surimi procurement, formulation R&D, manufacturing, distribution, branding, and direct retail—constitutes a rare and defensible competitive moat in the domestic fish cake industry. This structure enables the company to command roughly 2x the price premium of rivals while generating approximately 5x their revenue volume, underpinning five consecutive #1 rankings in the K-BPI seafood processing category. The fish cake bakery stores (domestic and overseas) function as experiential brand platforms that build consumer loyalty and drive a favorable high-ASP product mix, as evidenced by the consistent sell-out record of premium gift sets every holiday season. Vertical integration also provides structural advantages in new product development speed, quality control, and cost optimization relative to outsourcing-dependent peers, contributing to long-term market share defense.

RCPS Conversion Complete; Profitability on an Upward Trajectory

The complete conversion of RCPS to common equity in May 2025 eliminated recurring non-operational charges—interest expense and derivative valuation losses—directly driving the 410.6% net income surge in FY2025 and improving the visibility of future earnings quality. Operating margin has improved steadily from 2.6% (2023) to 5.5% (2025), driven by operating leverage from revenue growth and a favorable shift in product mix toward higher-margin items. The resolution of RCPS-related financial uncertainty simplifies the capital structure and removes a longstanding overhang on shareholder value, which is constructive for long-term investors. Should capacity expansion and further product mix improvement proceed as planned, there is meaningful room for the currently mid-single-digit operating margin to move higher.

Export revenues accounted for only 2.8% of total sales in 2024, representing a very substantial gap from the stated target of 30% within five years. Fish cake production is sensitive to surimi price fluctuations, and the fresh cold-chain model structurally elevates logistics and packaging costs. While gross margins (~42–44%) are relatively healthy, the scale of selling and administrative expenses limits the upside of operating leverage. As long as the operating margin remains in the mid-single-digit range, the stock's capacity to attract a higher valuation multiple is constrained.

Post-IPO Price Decline and Residual Lock-Up Overhang

Despite institutional lock-up commitment ratios of 77% at the time of listing, a graduated overhang structure—with tradeable float expanding at one-month, three-month, and twelve-month post-listing thresholds—continues to weigh on share supply-demand dynamics. Risk factors

  • Raw Material Price and FX Risk: Surimi (fish meat paste), the primary raw material for fish cake, is subject to material price volatility driven by marine resource availability, climate change, and global catch fluctuations; exchange rate movements also affect input costs even under the company's direct overseas procurement model. The FY2025 annual results disclosure explicitly cited rising exchange rates and raw material price increases as cost headwinds. Given the thin operating margin buffer of ~5%, a sharp spike in surimi prices would have a disproportionate impact on profitability. Growing overseas revenue exposure will also incrementally increase the company's currency risk profile over time.
  • International Market Entry and Localization Risk: Achieving the declared 30%-overseas-revenue-in-five-years target requires simultaneous wins in securing large-format distribution channels in the U.S., China, and Europe alongside product localization suited to local consumer preferences. The market acceptance of the new shelf-stable fish cake in overseas markets is unproven, and differentiation from competing local seafood processed foods such as Japanese kamaboko presents a real challenge. The IPO proceeds earmarked for overseas marketing amount to only approximately KRW 1.4 billion—a limited budget for meaningful brand-building in competitive international markets. Distribution partner negotiation delays, local food regulatory requirements, and cultural barriers to fish cake adoption all represent risks that could push back the global expansion timeline.
  • Production Efficiency and Capacity Expansion Execution Risk: Fish cake is a fresh product manufactured and shipped on the same day, meaning factory utilization regularly exceeds 140% during peak seasons (Chuseok and winter), leading to recurring capacity shortfalls and lost revenue opportunities. If the ongoing Jangrim factory expansion (+25% capacity) is delayed or fails to execute on schedule, peak-season revenue leakage will persist, and the transition from manual processes and aging equipment to automated lines carries inherent quality risk during the changeover period. As overseas order volumes scale, the single domestic production hub may increasingly face supply constraints, potentially necessitating earlier-than-planned investment in additional or overseas manufacturing capacity. Should accelerated capex commitments pressure near-term free cash flow, the company's financial flexibility could be temporarily reduced.

Bottom line

Samjin Food has built Korea's dominant fish cake brand position through 70 years of expertise, vertical integration, and a consistent premium repositioning strategy. The FY2025 DART-confirmed results—revenue growth of 13.6% to KRW 109.5 billion, operating margin improvement to 5.5%, and balance sheet normalization following RCPS conversion—point to gradually strengthening underlying fundamentals. However, the global expansion strategy remains at an early stage: the gap between the current overseas revenue share (~2.8%) and the stated 30% target is substantial, and execution risk is high across all international fronts. At the current share price of KRW 5,820 (23.4% below the IPO price), the IPO enthusiasm premium has been largely absorbed, but the residual lock-up overhang extending to December 2026 and the mid-single-digit operating margin continue to constrain a near-term valuation re-rating. Over the medium term, the decisive variables for the stock's trajectory will be the commercial validation of the shelf-stable fish cake product overseas, concrete distribution wins in major U.S. and Chinese channels, and the degree of margin improvement delivered post-capacity expansion at Jangrim.
