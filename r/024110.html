Business

IBK is a state-owned bank established in 1961 under the Industrial Bank of Korea Act, with a business model specialized in financing SMEs and small business owners. Its largest shareholder is the Korean government, with the Ministry of Economy and Finance holding about 59%, giving it a clear policy-bank character, while the Korea Development Bank and Export-Import Bank are also major shareholders. Its core revenue source is interest income from SME lending, followed by gains on securities valuation and disposal, fees and trust income. As of the end of Q1 2026, its SME loan balance stood at KRW 264.2tn, holding a market share around 24.4% and solidifying its position as the leading bank in SME finance. The group holds nine non-bank subsidiaries including IBK Capital and IBK Securities, and operates four overseas entities in China, Indonesia, Myanmar and Poland, plus nine overseas branches and two representative offices. In April 2026 it obtained final approval to establish a local subsidiary in Vietnam and is preparing to launch the Vietnam entity. Among subsidiaries, IBK Capital contributes the largest profit, while securities, pension insurance and savings-bank arms are relatively small. Competitively, it vies with the four major commercial banks and regional banks for corporate lending, but its dominant share in the dedicated SME market as a policy-finance institution is a key differentiator.

Earnings

On an annual basis, operating profit rose gently from about KRW 3.43tn in 2023 to roughly KRW 3.59tn in 2024 and KRW 3.66tn in 2025, while owners' net profit reached about KRW 2.71tn in 2025, slightly above the prior year's KRW 2.64tn. However, ROE declined gradually from 8.4% in 2023 to 7.8% in 2024 and 7.4% in 2025, as profit growth failed to keep pace with rising equity. Quarterly, owners' net profit slowed from about KRW 808.6bn in Q1 2025 to KRW 693.5bn in Q2 and KRW 462.5bn in Q4, before recovering to about KRW 748.7bn in Q1 2026. IBK reported Q1 consolidated net profit of KRW 753.4bn, down 7.5% from a year earlier. This was attributed to the high-base effect of a record-large prior-year first quarter and to FX variables stemming from the Middle East conflict. Still, core banking interest income reached about KRW 1.85tn in Q1, up 3.8%, as it expanded interest income by cutting funding costs despite wider FX losses. Net interest margin (NIM) rose 3bp quarter on quarter to 1.60%, attributed to a combination of higher market rates and funding-cost reduction efforts. On asset quality, the substandard-and-below loan ratio held at a manageable 1.28%, while the credit-cost ratio fell 4bp from the prior year-end to 0.43%. The FX losses are largely non-monetary accounting losses, making them a one-off variable that could reverse into an earnings tailwind if the won strengthens.

Industry

Korea's banking sector sits at a turning point in the rate cycle. The Bank of Korea held its policy rate at 2.5% at its May 2026 meeting, marking an eighth consecutive hold despite being in an easing cycle. Rate cuts and the open possibility of further cuts act to compress net interest margins. Amid government household-debt volume control and tightened mortgage rules, corporate lending will grow under the 'productive finance transformation' stance, but aggressive expansion is limited by asset-quality management. Through Q3 2025, general-bank net profit rose on improved non-interest income, but NIM stagnated and total loan growth slowed, weakening earnings power. There are also views that the shift from mortgages to SME loans under productive finance is positive for profitability in the short term. This structural factor can work relatively in favor of IBK, which specializes in SME lending. Still, considering rising corporate loans from stronger productive finance and external factors such as a higher won-dollar rate, asset-quality metrics face structural downward pressure.

Outlook

The company is moving along two axes: core competitiveness and revamping shareholder returns. IBK said it will keep supporting SMEs struggling with higher FX and oil prices via its 'IBK-style productive finance 30-300 project,' and plans to introduce quarterly dividends for the first time with July 31 as the record date. Over the year since announcing its value-up plan, it expanded shareholder returns and amended its articles of incorporation to enable quarterly dividends, aiming to escape market undervaluation. For fiscal 2025, the dividend per share is KRW 1,048, total dividends are KRW 835.7bn, and the payout ratio is around 35%. Its payout ratio is linked to the CET1 ratio, with a maximum of 35% in the band up to 12%, and the CET1 ratio at the end of last year was 11.48%. Because it keeps expanding risk-weighted assets, the denominator of the capital ratio, through stronger public functions, improving the CET1 ratio is not easy. To qualify for separate dividend-income taxation, the consolidated payout ratio must exceed 40%, but it currently stands around 31%, so analysts see near-term qualification as difficult. The launch of the Vietnam entity and other global expansion, plus improved subsidiary profitability, will be variables for medium- to long-term growth.

Valuation

IBK is a representative low-PBR financial stock trading at a steep discount to net assets. Since announcing its value-up plan, its price-to-book ratio broke out of the 0.30x range it had occupied for years, but it still sits around 0.44x, leaving room cited for further improvement. On a headline basis, return on equity is about 7.5%, earnings per share are KRW 3,099, and book value per share is KRW 46,146, reflecting a relationship where net assets substantially exceed market capitalization. Its dividend yield is assessed in the 5% range, higher than commercial banks' roughly 3%, but the gap could narrow if competitors gain access to separate dividend-income taxation. Because share buybacks and cancellations are not used as a return tool and it does not meet the requirements for capital-reduction dividends, there is a coexisting view that its appeal is relatively weak versus peer bank stocks in the variety of return measures. In short, it can be seen as a zone that reflects both the quantitative appeal of a discount to net assets and the qualitative limits of capital and dividend constraints unique to a state-owned bank.
