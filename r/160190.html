Business

Higen RNM is a motor and robotics drive-solution specialist that began as LG Electronics' motor division in 1963 and became independent in 2008 by acquiring Otis Elevator Korea's industrial motor unit. Its business spans general-purpose motors, servo motors/drives, robotic actuators and EV drive modules. As of Q1 2025, segment revenue mix breaks down into general-purpose motors at 82.1%, servo motors/actuators at 12.6%, and EV motors at 5.3%. For 2024, around 79.4% of total revenue came from the general-purpose motor segment. General-purpose motors are industrial AC motors used to drive pumps, fans and compressors; the company ranks third domestically with roughly a 10% share behind Hyosung Heavy Industries and HD Hyundai Electric, with the top three controlling over 80% of the market. It operates production sites in Changwon (Korea) and Qingdao (China) and was the first in Korea to localize a 3kW explosion-proof servo motor. The servo segment targets industrial customers such as collaborative robots, logistics robots (AMR), semiconductors and batteries, while EV mobility supplies drive modules for golf carts, buses, agricultural machinery and small EVs not addressed by automakers. The company is considered the only domestic firm able to supply fully in-house smart actuators, designing and mass-producing motor, drive, reducer and servo by itself.

Earnings

Earnings clearly reflect the cost burden of a company in transition. In 2024, consolidated revenue was about 75.89 billion won, with an operating loss of roughly 0.37 billion won and an owners' net loss of about 1.08 billion won, an operating margin of around -0.5%. In 2025, revenue edged down to about 73.54 billion won while the operating loss widened sharply to roughly 8.91 billion won and the owners' net loss grew to about 7.06 billion won, pushing the operating margin to -12.1%. Operating cash flow also deteriorated, from about -2.63 billion won in 2024 to about -9.69 billion won in 2025. By quarter in 2025, revenue and operating loss ran at 18.17bn/2.38bn in Q1, 16.71bn/1.39bn in Q2, 15.88bn/2.09bn in Q3 and 22.78bn/3.05bn in Q4 (won), keeping losses persistent. In Q1 2026 revenue recovered year-on-year to 18.63 billion won, but the operating loss widened to 3.39 billion won and the owners' net loss to 3.21 billion won. Management attributed the persistent losses to falling general-purpose motor orders, and the fact that operating profit, slightly positive before listing, has widened its loss every quarter since the IPO is a concern. A construction-sector slump cutting motor demand, a worsening product mix, and continued robot-actuator R&D spending are cited as drivers of the operating loss. The debt ratio improved from 75.8% in 2024 to 55.7% in 2025.

Industry

The company straddles two end-markets: a stable industrial-motor market and a high-growth robotics and humanoid market. The end-markets for general-purpose motors, pumps, compressors and blowers, show steady annual growth, but recently revenue has fallen and profitability has worsened amid shifting market conditions and intensifying competition. In Korea's general-purpose motor market, a phased minimum-efficiency standard moving from IE3 to IE4 is expected to generate replacement demand. As mandatory IE4 standards roll out in stages from 2026, replacement demand for IE4 motors should rise, accompanied by price increases since IE4 motors cost about 25% more than IE3. In robotics, it shares a component value chain and competes and cooperates with reducer and servo developers such as SPG, SBB Tech and Samhyun. Industrial robots use 3 to 6 actuators each, but humanoid robots use 18 to 75, so the actuator market grows explosively as the humanoid market expands. The company is also cited as a potential beneficiary of possible Chinese rare-earth export restrictions. The key sector-wide risk remains the still-uncertain timing of humanoid mass production.

Outlook

The outlook hinges on a gradual recovery in general-purpose motors and whether the actuator business takes off. Brokerages project 2026 revenue of 81.8 billion won (up 9.0%) and operating profit of 0.6 billion won (a return to profit), expecting general-purpose motor revenue to grow over 9% on rising replacement demand from energy-efficiency rules and a bottoming of construction and capex. The company is investing about 12 billion won to add an automated smart-actuator line on the second floor of its Changwon headquarters, currently holding about 130 billion won of annual capacity, with further expansion planned as orders materialize. However, the expansion originally slated for December 2025 has reportedly been pushed somewhat to the first half of 2026 to align with the robotics market's takeoff. The Ministry of Trade, Industry and Energy's "next-generation smart actuator" project is expected to complete product development in the first half of 2026, with marketing launched upon completion. Once the plant is finished, the company is expected to make supply proposals to major domestic robot firms such as Doosan, Hanwha and LG, particularly targeting new humanoid players. Brokerages estimate the combined servo motor, servo drive, actuator and SI revenue share rising from 17% in 2024 to 19% in 2025 and 25% in 2026. These projections, however, depend heavily on the pace of the robotics market's emergence, leaving substantial timing uncertainty.

Bull case

Vertically integrated humanoid actuators

The company is regarded as the only domestic firm able to design and mass-produce motor, drive, reducer and servo in-house. When humanoids commercialize, actuator orders could surge and reshape the revenue mix, with an average of 25-30 actuators per humanoid and up to 60 for premium models. If the robotics market truly takes off, this vertical integration could become key leverage.

IE4 regulation-driven replacement cycle

As mandatory IE4 standards phase in domestically from 2026, IE4 motor replacement demand should rise, with price increases as IE4 motors cost about 25% more than IE3. This is a structural catalyst that can lift both volume and unit price of the cash-cow general-purpose motor business. The company is leading a national project to develop super-premium efficiency motors and plans to build mass-production capacity by 2026.

Proven motor expertise and references

Higen RNM is an actuator specialist with roughly 60 years of accumulated expertise, having started as LG Electronics' motor division in 1963. It is building references by supplying servo motors for R&D to major domestic robot OEMs. It has been named a Korean Robot Company of the Year for seven consecutive years from 2019 to 2025, confirming its standing in the industry.

Bear case

Widening losses and cash burn

The 2025 operating loss widened sharply to about 8.91 billion won and the operating margin fell to -12.1%. Operating cash flow also deteriorated to roughly -9.69 billion won. The fact that operating profit, positive on a quarterly basis before listing, has widened its loss every quarter since the IPO weighs on the fundamentals.

Risk of delayed robotics market takeoff

The fact that the robotics market has yet to take off is a burden; costs only fall with mass-production supply, but with the market still not open, robot companies are all posting losses. Humanoid mass production is being delayed, leaving uncertain the timing at which expectations translate into earnings. The expansion schedule itself has already been pushed back to align with the robotics market's emergence.

Dependence on end-cycle and intensifying competition

A construction-sector slump cutting general-purpose motor demand and a worsening product mix were direct causes of the operating loss. With about 80% of revenue concentrated in cyclical general-purpose motors, a delayed end-market recovery could slow the rebound. In robotics too, domestic and global players such as Doosan Robotics and Rainbow Robotics are entering, intensifying competition.
