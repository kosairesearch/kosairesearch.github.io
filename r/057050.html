Business

Hyundai Home Shopping is a non-store retailer whose core operations are TV home shopping and the Hmall online mall, listed on the KOSPI in 2010. Its consolidated results incorporate fashion affiliate Handsome, digital-signage and IT firm Hyundai Future Net, and building-materials maker Hyundai L&C as subsidiaries or equity-method entities. The consolidation of Hyundai Future Net and Handsome as subsidiaries in 2024 sharply expanded the top line. Hyundai Home Shopping holds roughly 39.7% of Handsome and 48% of Hyundai Future Net. The core home-shopping operation leverages the Hyundai Department Store brand to lift the share of higher-margin food, fashion and beauty products. On a standalone basis in 2024, revenue was about KRW 1,092.6bn and operating profit about KRW 61.8bn, as cost efficiency and a higher-margin mix lifted profitability. The competitive field comprises seven TV home-shopping operators, including GS Shop, CJ Onstyle, Lotte Homeshopping, NS Homeshopping, Home&Shopping and Public Shopping. The company is strengthening its function as a shopping portal through mobile video-shopping features such as 'Shora' (Shopping Live). In the group structure, Hyundai Home Shopping had served as an intermediate holding entity under Hyundai GF Holdings, overseeing Handsome, Hyundai Future Net and Hyundai L&C.

Earnings

In 2024, consolidated revenue was about KRW 3,853.5bn and operating profit about KRW 130.1bn as the Handsome and Hyundai Future Net consolidation greatly expanded the top line, though the operating margin was just 3.4%. In 2025, consolidated revenue was about KRW 3,789.8bn, operating profit about KRW 130.8bn and owner net profit about KRW 99.0bn, with net profit declining year on year and an operating margin of around 3.5%. The net-profit decline reflects a base effect against the relatively high 2024 result (owner net profit of about KRW 157.7bn). By quarter, 2025 operating profit eased from about KRW 48.1bn in Q1 to the KRW 25-30bn range in Q2-Q4, before rebounding sharply to about KRW 65.3bn in Q1 2026. Q1 2026 revenue rose 1.9% YoY to KRW 978.49bn and operating profit climbed 35.9% to KRW 65.34bn. In the core business, an expanded mix of higher-margin broadcast products lifted standalone Q1 revenue 4.7% to KRW 281.3bn and operating profit 9% to KRW 27.8bn. Operating cash flow recovered from a negative figure in 2022 to about KRW 250.3bn in 2024 and KRW 213.5bn in 2025, indicating steady cash generation. Through the first three quarters of 2025, weakness at subsidiaries such as Hyundai L&C and Handsome dragged both revenue and operating profit lower, while the core home-shopping operation partly recovered profitability on a larger share of high-margin products. The debt-to-equity ratio fell from 42.0% in 2022 to 32.5% in 2025, leaving the balance sheet relatively stable.

Industry

The TV home-shopping industry is widely seen as entering a structural decline in both scale and profitability. Combined 2025 transaction volume for the seven operators fell 5.1% to KRW 18,505bn, the fifth straight annual decline since KRW 21,977.1bn in 2021. Broadcast revenue dropped to KRW 2,618bn, the lowest level since 2012. Broadcast-carriage fees edged down 1.1% to KRW 1,915.3bn, yet their share of broadcast revenue stood at 73.2%, up 13.3 percentage points in four years from 59.9% in 2021. A government revision to fee guidelines has effectively stalled since being transferred to the Korea Communications Commission, adding to industry uncertainty. Still, Hyundai Home Shopping has fared relatively better than peers thanks to cost efficiency and higher-margin programming. The industry is seeking a way out through same-day and instant delivery and logistics automation; Hyundai Home Shopping has installed AI-based robotic arms and a singulator at its Hwaseong logistics center to raise throughput and cut shipping time. Heavier broadcast regulation and high carriage fees relative to live commerce and e-commerce remain industry-wide burdens.

Outlook

The most important schedule item is the governance overhaul. Hyundai GF Holdings is taking Hyundai Home Shopping in as a 100% subsidiary via a stock swap—the first such case since the revised commercial code—and the move is expected to ease the holding-company discount. The swap is set for June 30, after which Hyundai Home Shopping shares will no longer trade, with delisting to follow on July 20. The swap ratio is about 6.357 GF Holdings shares per Hyundai Home Shopping share, so a 100-share holding converts to roughly 635 shares. After the swap, Hyundai Home Shopping will be split into an investment company holding Handsome, Hyundai Future Net and Hyundai L&C, and an operating company pursuing the core home-shopping business plus new ventures and M&A, with the new investment entity later merging into Hyundai GF Holdings. On the business front, the company plans to broaden online and offline sales channels and strengthen senior-targeted operations as it transitions into a comprehensive commerce company. It is also pursuing private-brand expansion to secure long-term revenue sources. The key change post-delisting is that Hyundai Home Shopping will cease to be a standalone listed entity disclosing its own price and earnings and will be integrated as part of Hyundai GF Holdings.

Valuation

Valuation here is dominated, unlike a typical listed company, by the imminent delisting and stock-swap structure. The stock traded at a steep discount to net asset value for an extended period, and the company's headline return on equity is 4.6%, earnings per share are KRW 9,523 and book value per share is about KRW 209,997. The appraisal-rights buyback price was set at KRW 60,709, and the current market price sits above that buyback level. After the June 30 swap, an independent share price will disappear and value will track the roughly 6.357 exchange ratio and the GF Holdings share price, so conventional PER, PBR and dividend-yield comparisons carry limited meaning. For reference, the most recent year-end dividend was KRW 2,800 per common share. One should also weigh that, while past revenue expanded without losses, the shares had retreated to levels below early post-listing prices amid stagnation in the core business.

Bull case

Absorbing the firm as a wholly owned subsidiary to remove the dual-listing simplifies a structure long valued below the sum of its affiliate stakes. With the dual-listing gone, the value of the affiliates the holding company owns can be reflected more directly in the share price. Analysts expect higher dividends and value uplift from simplified group decision-making following the full-subsidiary integration.

Stronger sales of higher-margin food and fashion products lifted both standalone revenue and operating profit.

Stable finances and cash generation

The debt-to-equity ratio improved from 42.0% in 2022 to 32.5% in 2025, strengthening financial health. Operating cash flow was robust at about KRW 250.3bn in 2024 and KRW 213.5bn in 2025. Bear case

Structural industry slump

The shrinking scale of the TV home-shopping industry is unlikely to reverse quickly. Total transaction volume has fallen for five straight years since 2021, with a five-year CAGR of minus 4.2%. Falling TV viewership and a shift in consumption toward online shopping platforms have weighed on the business.

Heavy carriage-fee burden

Carriage fees are the single largest cost item and remain hard to shrink even as revenue falls. Their share of broadcast revenue stands at a still-high 73.2%, and the cost-burden structure has not improved much even amid declining sales. A government guideline revision to adjust them is also delayed, increasing uncertainty.

Loss of liquidity from delisting

After the swap, Hyundai Home Shopping shares no longer trade independently. Following June 30, the shares cease trading and the company delists on July 20. Holders convert into GF Holdings shareholders at the set ratio, so the effective investment subject shifts to the holding company's share price.
